Evaluation Report: GazeParser

Use of GazeParser in Libraries and Workplaces

GazeParser is an open-source eye-tracking toolkit written in Python. It can be used in libraries or educational institutions to study user interactions with digital interfaces. For example, it can help assess how patrons search for information in online catalogs or use digital kiosks, providing insights into usability issues and design improvements.

Interesting Uses in Library Contexts

One interesting use of GazeParser is in academic libraries, where it can track how students interact with digital learning environments. Another use is in public libraries, where it can assist in evaluating touchscreen interfaces for accessibility. Studies like those published in the Journal of Information Science often highlight such applications of eye-tracking tools.

Current Usage and Popularity

GazeParser is hosted on GitHub and has over 100 stars and several forks, with an active but niche user base in the HCI and research community. It's cited in academic papers and widely used in HCI experiments and psychology studies. As a Python-based tool, it's also supported by a broad ecosystem of developers and researchers.

General Pros and Cons of Open-Source Tools

Open-source tools are cost-effective, customizable, and benefit from community-driven development. However, they often lack formal technical support, may have limited documentation, and require more technical knowledge to deploy. Security concerns and compatibility issues can also be a downside.

Advantages and Disadvantages of GazeParser

GazeParser offers a flexible framework for eye-tracking research with support for affordable eye trackers like EyeTribe. It's ideal for academic environments. However, it requires basic programming skills, has a dated interface, and lacks a plug-and-play setup. The community is small but supportive, and updates are relatively infrequent.
